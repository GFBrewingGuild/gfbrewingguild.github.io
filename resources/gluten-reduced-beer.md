# Gluten-Reduced Beer

## Summary for consumers, retailers, distributors, and hospitality teams

Gluten-reduced beer made from barley, wheat, rye, or related gluten-containing grains is **not the same thing as naturally gluten-free beer**.

For people with celiac disease or medical reasons to avoid gluten, this distinction matters. Beers made from gluten-containing grains and treated with enzymes to reduce measurable gluten may still contain gluten protein fragments that are difficult to detect reliably with common tests.

GFBG recommends that beer labeled, marketed, shelved, or served as gluten-free should be made from **100% naturally gluten-free ingredients**.

Examples of naturally gluten-free brewing grains and ingredients include millet, buckwheat, rice, sorghum, maize, quinoa, and certified gluten-free oats where legally permitted and appropriate.

## Key terms

### Naturally gluten-free beer

Naturally gluten-free beer is made only from ingredients that are originally free from gluten-containing grains. This means no barley, wheat, rye, triticale, spelt, or related gluten-containing cereals are used in the recipe.

This is the gluten-free brewing best practice supported by GFBG.

### Gluten-reduced, gluten-removed, low-gluten, or deglutinized beer

Gluten-reduced beer is typically brewed from barley or other gluten-containing grains and then treated with an enzyme or process intended to break down gluten proteins.

These beers may also be marketed with terms such as:

* Gluten-reduced
* Gluten-removed
* Crafted to remove gluten
* Low gluten
* Deglutinized

The exact wording varies by country and may be regulated differently in different jurisdictions.

GFBG prefers the term **gluten-reduced** rather than “gluten-removed,” because “removed” can misleadingly suggest that gluten is completely absent.

## Why gluten-reduced beer is controversial

Most gluten-reduced beers are made from barley. Brewers commonly add a prolyl endopeptidase enzyme, often known by product names such as Clarex or Clarity Ferm, to break down proteins during fermentation.

This approach was originally developed to reduce haze in conventional barley-based beer. It later became a method for producing beers that test below certain gluten thresholds using some testing methods.

The concern is that breaking proteins into smaller fragments is not the same as eliminating all celiac-reactive gluten sequences.

## The main safety issue

Gluten proteins are made from chains of amino acids. Some patterns in those chains, called epitopes, can trigger an immune response in people with celiac disease.

Enzymes used in gluten-reduced beer can fragment gluten proteins, but they may not reliably destroy every immunogenic epitope of concern.

This creates a major problem: a beer may test below a threshold using a specific antibody-based test while still containing gluten-derived fragments that may be relevant to people with celiac disease.

## Testing limitations

Many gluten-reduced beers rely on ELISA testing. ELISA tests look for specific target sequences. However, hydrolyzed and fermented products such as beer are difficult to test accurately because the proteins have been broken into fragments.

A key distinction is:

* **Antigenic epitopes** are sequences detected by a test.
* **Immunogenic epitopes** are sequences that may trigger an immune response in the body.

A test may fail to detect some fragments that remain relevant to celiac safety.

This is why several regulators and researchers have raised concerns about using standard gluten testing methods to verify gluten content in fermented or hydrolyzed foods and beverages.

## Why “below 20 ppm” can be misleading

The phrase “below 20 ppm” is often used in gluten-free food labeling. For intact gluten in many foods, this threshold is widely recognized.

For gluten-reduced beer, however, the issue is not only the number. The issue is whether the test can reliably identify and quantify hydrolyzed gluten fragments in a fermented product.

A barley-based beer treated with enzymes may produce a low result on one test while still containing gluten-derived protein fragments that are not fully captured by that test.

For consumers with celiac disease, this makes “tested below 20 ppm” an incomplete and potentially misleading safety claim when applied to gluten-reduced barley beer.

## Best practice

The best practice for gluten-free beer is simple:

> Use 100% naturally gluten-free ingredients from the start.

This avoids the central uncertainty of trying to break down, measure, and validate residual gluten fragments in a barley-based product.

Naturally gluten-free beer also supports a dedicated supply chain for gluten-free grains, maltsters, brewers, retailers, and consumers.

## Country-by-country labeling notes

Labeling laws vary significantly by country. The following summaries are intended as general guidance, not legal advice.

### United States

In the United States, beer labeling is regulated by the Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau with reference to FDA rules.

Gluten-free beer must be made from ingredients that are naturally free of gluten. Beers made from gluten-containing grains and treated to reduce gluten cannot simply be labeled gluten-free. They may use wording such as “processed,” “treated,” or “crafted” to remove gluten, subject to specific labeling rules.

### Canada

In Canada, beer made from barley, wheat, rye, or triticale cannot make a gluten-free claim simply because additional processing steps were used to reduce gluten.

Beer-like products made from non-gluten grains may use a gluten-free claim if they meet the relevant requirements and are protected from cross-contact.

### Australia and New Zealand

Australia and New Zealand have especially strict gluten-free labeling rules. Gluten-free claims require no detectable gluten.

Barley-based beers treated to reduce gluten may be sold as low-gluten where permitted, but they are not considered suitable for people with celiac disease.

### Argentina

Argentina requires gluten-free products to be made without wheat, oats, barley, or rye and to meet its gluten-free standard. Barley-based gluten-reduced beer does not meet the same naturally gluten-free expectation.

### United Kingdom and Europe

The United Kingdom and European Union have historically allowed some barley-based beers that test below a threshold to be marketed as gluten-free. This has created confusion because consumers may see barley-based enzyme-treated beers and naturally gluten-free beers presented as equivalent.

GFBG considers this distinction important. A beer made from barley and treated to reduce gluten is not equivalent to a beer brewed from 100% naturally gluten-free ingredients.

## Low-gluten barley

Some plant breeding work has explored barley varieties with lower levels of certain gluten protein families. One example is Kebari, a low-gluten barley developed by CSIRO in Australia.

Low-gluten barley is different from conventional barley treated with enzymes, but it still raises important labeling, safety, cross-contact, and consumer-trust questions.

For GFBG purposes, low-gluten barley is not considered part of 100% naturally gluten-free brewing.

## The role of celiac and coeliac associations

Celiac associations can play an important role in helping consumers understand the difference between naturally gluten-free beer and gluten-reduced beer.

Helpful actions include:

* Clearly distinguishing naturally gluten-free beer from gluten-reduced barley beer
* Encouraging transparent beer labeling
* Communicating current research on hydrolyzed and fermented gluten testing
* Supporting brewers who use naturally gluten-free ingredients
* Promoting dedicated gluten-free grain, malting, and brewing supply chains

## Guidance for retailers and hospitality teams

Retailers, bars, restaurants, and distributors should not treat gluten-reduced beer and naturally gluten-free beer as interchangeable.

Best practices include:

* Do not shelve gluten-reduced barley beer as equivalent to naturally gluten-free beer.
* Do not describe gluten-reduced beer as celiac-safe.
* Make ingredient differences clear on menus and tap lists.
* Train staff to understand the distinction.
* When in doubt, identify the grain source.

A simple consumer-facing distinction is:

> Naturally gluten-free beer is brewed without gluten-containing grains. Gluten-reduced beer usually starts with barley and is treated later.

## Guidance for brewers

Brewers who want to serve people with celiac disease should prioritize naturally gluten-free brewing methods.

This means:

* Using only naturally gluten-free fermentables
* Preventing cross-contact with barley, wheat, rye, and related grains
* Understanding applicable labeling rules
* Being transparent with consumers
* Avoiding vague terms such as “gluten friendly”
